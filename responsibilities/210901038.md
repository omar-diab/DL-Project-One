## Responsibilities and Contributions

My primary responsibility in this project was the design, implementation, and documentation of the **regularization analysis component** within the broader experimental pipeline. More specifically, I developed a **controlled comparative framework** to investigate how different regularization strategies influence overfitting, generalization, and training stability in an MLP trained on CIFAR-10.

Rather than approaching regularization methods as independent implementation choices, this component was structured as a **systematic ablation study**. The central methodological principle was to keep the experimental conditions fixed and vary only the regularization strategy, so that any observed performance differences could be attributed directly to the method under investigation rather than to uncontrolled training variations.

### 1. Design of the controlled experimental setup

A major part of my contribution was the design of a **fair and scientifically interpretable experimental setting** for the regularization branch. To ensure comparability across experiments, I fixed the following elements throughout the study:

- the train / validation / test split,
- the model architecture,
- the optimizer,
- the learning rate,
- the batch size,
- the epoch budget,
- and the random seed.

This design choice was essential for transforming the regularization component into a valid comparative study. By removing confounding factors, the analysis could focus directly on the role of the regularization mechanism itself.

In addition, the study deliberately began with an **overfitting-prone baseline**. The use of a relatively high-capacity MLP together with a limited training subset and the absence of regularization created a clear reference point against which the effect of each regularization strategy could be evaluated.

### 2. Implementation of multiple regularization families

I was responsible for implementing and organizing several regularization methods drawn from different conceptual categories, so that the analysis would capture not only performance differences but also methodological differences between regularization mechanisms.

The implemented methods included:

- **Dropout** as a form of stochastic regularization,
- **Early Stopping** as an implicit regularization mechanism,
- **Batch Normalization** as an architectural / normalization-based regularizer,
- **L2 regularization** interpreted explicitly through the lens of MAP estimation with a Gaussian prior.

In addition to individual methods, I also implemented combined configurations in order to study possible interactions between complementary strategies:

- **Dropout + BatchNorm**
- **L2 + Dropout**

All of these configurations were integrated into the same backbone architecture and evaluated under the same controlled setup, ensuring methodological consistency across experiments.

### 3. Theoretical framing of L2 regularization as MAP estimation

One of the most important conceptual contributions of my work was to present **L2 regularization** not merely as a practical weight decay term, but as a **Bayesian Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem** under a Gaussian prior.

The training objective was explicitly formulated as:

\[
\mathcal{L}_{MAP} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \lambda \sum_i w_i^2
\]

This interpretation establishes a direct connection between implementation and theory:

- the cross-entropy term corresponds to the negative log-likelihood,
- the L2 penalty corresponds to the negative log-prior,
- and the combined objective corresponds to MAP estimation.

By making this interpretation explicit in both the code and the written documentation, I aimed to ensure that this part of the project was theoretically grounded rather than treated as a purely heuristic modification.

### 4. Extension of the training and evaluation pipeline

I also extended the training pipeline so that it could support **flexible yet comparable experimentation** across multiple regularization settings. This included:

- enabling or disabling regularization components through configuration,
- tracking multiple loss components where relevant,
- computing train / validation / test accuracy,
- computing generalization gaps,
- and supporting early stopping with proper checkpoint restoration.

A key goal here was to ensure that all experiments produced outputs in a consistent format, which made later comparison, aggregation, and interpretation significantly more reliable.

### 5. Logging, persistence, and reproducibility

Another important part of my contribution concerned **experiment persistence and reproducibility**. During development, runtime resets made it clear that results could easily be lost unless the workflow was designed to save them systematically.

To address this, I implemented a persistent logging mechanism that stored:

- full training histories,
- per-epoch metrics,
- final summary metrics,
- and relevant experiment metadata.

This design separated **training** from **analysis**, which made it possible to revisit results, compare experiments, and generate plots without retraining models from scratch. It also improved the robustness of the overall workflow.

### 6. Visualization and comparative analysis

I was additionally responsible for the visualization and comparison layer of the regularization branch. The goal was not only to run experiments, but also to make their behavior analytically interpretable.

This included the development of:

- per-experiment training curves,
- summary tables across methods,
- and comparison plots in which the baseline and regularized models were visualized on the same scale.

These outputs made it possible to examine:

- overfitting behavior,
- training stability,
- generalization improvements,
- and the relative effect of individual versus combined regularization strategies.

In this sense, my role extended beyond implementation to the **analytical presentation** of the experimental findings.

### 7. Repository-level organization and technical documentation

Beyond the branch-specific regularization experiments, I also contributed to the **overall structure and presentation of the repository**. In practice, I helped shape the project so that it would appear as a coherent and technically documented study rather than as a disconnected collection of notebooks.

This contribution included preparing and organizing the main documentation files, particularly:

- **`README.md`**
- **`report.md` / `report.pdf`**

I also played a central role in giving the repository its final form, ensuring that the experimental content, folder structure, documentation, and presentation style were aligned. This was particularly important because the project was evaluated not only in terms of implementation, but also in terms of **technical documentation, methodological depth, and clarity of presentation**.

### 8. Overall contribution

Taken together, my contribution consisted in building a complete and reproducible framework for the **regularization side of the project**, spanning methodology design, implementation, theoretical grounding, experiment management, analysis, visualization, and documentation.

In concrete terms, this contribution included:

- the design of an intentionally overfitting baseline,
- the construction of a controlled comparison framework,
- the implementation of multiple regularization methods and combinations,
- the theoretical formulation of L2 regularization as MAP estimation,
- the extension of the training and evaluation workflow,
- persistent logging and result management,
- comparative visualization and analysis,
- and the preparation of the main project documentation together with the overall repository organization.

Overall, my work addressed the following central question:

**How do different regularization strategies affect overfitting and generalization when all other training conditions are kept constant, and how can these findings be integrated into a coherent, technically documented deep learning project?**

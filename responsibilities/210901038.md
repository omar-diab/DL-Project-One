## Responsibilities and Contributions

My main responsibility in this project was to design and implement the **regularization analysis component** within the overall pipeline. Specifically, I developed a controlled experimental framework to systematically evaluate how different regularization techniques affect overfitting and generalization on an MLP trained on CIFAR-10.

Rather than treating regularization methods as independent add-ons, I approached this task as a **structured ablation study**, where all training conditions are fixed and only the regularization method varies. This ensures that any observed differences in performance can be directly attributed to the regularization strategy.

### 1. Designing the controlled experimental setup

I was responsible for ensuring that all experiments were conducted under identical conditions. This included fixing:

- the train / validation / test split,
- the model architecture,
- the optimizer,
- the learning rate,
- the batch size,
- the number of training epochs,
- and the random seed.

The goal was to eliminate confounding factors and make the comparison between methods scientifically valid.

Additionally, Adel intentionally constructed an **overfitting baseline** by:

- using a relatively high-capacity MLP,
- limiting the size of the training data,
- and avoiding any regularization in the baseline.

This setup created a clear reference point, making it possible to observe how each method reduces overfitting.

---

### 2. Implementing multiple regularization families

I implemented regularization methods from **different conceptual categories**, ensuring that the analysis reflects their fundamentally different mechanisms:

- **Dropout** as stochastic regularization,  
- **Early Stopping** as implicit regularization,  
- **Batch Normalization** as architectural / normalization-based regularization,  
- **L2 regularization** interpreted explicitly as **MAP estimation with a Gaussian prior**.  

I also implemented combined configurations:

- Dropout + BatchNorm  
- L2 (MAP Gaussian Prior) + Dropout  

Each method was integrated into the same baseline architecture, ensuring consistency across experiments.

---

### 3. Interpreting L2 regularization as MAP estimation

A key part of my contribution was to present L2 regularization not only as weight decay, but as a **Bayesian MAP estimation problem with a Gaussian prior**.

The training objective was explicitly formulated as:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{MAP} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \lambda \sum_i w_i^2
$$

This interpretation connects implementation to theory:

- Cross-entropy corresponds to the negative log-likelihood,  
- the L2 term corresponds to the negative log-prior,  
- and the combined objective corresponds to MAP estimation.  

By explicitly incorporating and logging the L2 contribution during training, I ensured that this interpretation is both theoretically grounded and empirically observable.

---

### 4. Extending the training pipeline

I extended the training pipeline to support flexible experimentation with regularization. This included:

- enabling/disabling regularization methods through configuration,  
- tracking:
  - cross-entropy loss,  
  - explicit L2 contribution,  
  - total loss,  
- computing:
  - train / validation / test accuracy,  
  - generalization gaps,  
- supporting early stopping with proper checkpoint restoration.  

The pipeline was designed so that **all experiments produce comparable outputs**, making downstream analysis reliable and consistent.

---

### 5. Logging, persistence, and reproducibility

During development, runtime resets revealed a key issue: training results could be lost if not saved. To address this, I implemented a **persistent logging mechanism**.

This included:

- saving full training histories to disk,  
- storing per-epoch metrics (loss, accuracy, L2 contribution),  
- saving final metrics and experiment metadata.  

This design separates **training from analysis**, allowing experiments to be reproduced and compared without retraining, and making the workflow robust to environment resets.

---

### 6. Visualization and comparison

I developed reusable plotting utilities and a comparison workflow to analyze the results effectively.

This included:

- per-experiment training curves,  
- a unified summary table of all experiments,  
- comparison plots where the **baseline is visualized together with all regularized models on the same graph**.  

These visualizations allow direct observation of:

- overfitting behavior,  
- training stability,  
- generalization improvements,  
- and differences between individual and combined methods.  

---

### 7. Organization and integration

I organized this component of the project into a modular and reusable structure:

- shared utilities under `src/` (data loading, model, training, plotting),  
- separate experiment notebooks,  
- a centralized comparison workflow,  
- saved splits, histories, figures, and summary tables.  

This structure ensures clarity, reproducibility, and ease of evaluation.

## Responsibilities and Contributions

This contribution focused on the **design, implementation, and documentation of the regularization analysis component** within the broader project pipeline. The aim of this component was to construct a **controlled comparative framework** for examining how different regularization strategies influence overfitting, generalization, and training stability in an MLP trained on CIFAR-10.

Rather than treating regularization methods as isolated implementation choices, this part of the project was structured as a **systematic ablation study**. The underlying methodological principle was to keep the experimental conditions fixed while varying only the regularization strategy, so that any observed performance differences could be interpreted as effects of the method itself rather than artifacts of inconsistent training settings.

### 1. Design of the controlled experimental framework

A central aspect of this contribution was the design of a **scientifically interpretable comparison setting** for the regularization branch. To ensure comparability across experiments, the following elements were fixed throughout the main study:

- the train / validation / test split,
- the model architecture,
- the optimizer,
- the learning rate,
- the batch size,
- the epoch budget,
- and the random seed.

This design was essential for methodological validity. By eliminating confounding factors, the analysis could focus directly on the role of the regularization mechanism under investigation.

In addition, the regularization study was built around an **intentionally overfitting baseline**. The use of a relatively high-capacity MLP, together with a restricted training subset and the absence of regularization, created a clear reference point from which the effect of each regularization method could be evaluated.

### 2. Implementation of multiple regularization families

Another major component of the work involved implementing regularization methods drawn from different conceptual categories, so that the analysis would reflect not only differences in performance but also differences in mechanism.

The implemented methods included:

- **Dropout** as stochastic regularization,
- **Early Stopping** as implicit regularization,
- **Batch Normalization** as architectural / normalization-based regularization,
- **L2 regularization** interpreted explicitly through the framework of MAP estimation with a Gaussian prior.

In addition to the individual methods, combined configurations were also implemented in order to study potential interactions between complementary strategies:

- **Dropout + BatchNorm**
- **L2 + Dropout**

All configurations were integrated into the same baseline architecture and evaluated under the same controlled setup, thereby ensuring consistency across experiments.

### 3. Theoretical formulation of L2 regularization as MAP estimation

A particularly important conceptual contribution of this work was the interpretation of **L2 regularization** not merely as a practical weight decay term, but as a **Bayesian Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem** under a Gaussian prior.

The objective was explicitly formulated as:

\[
\mathcal{L}_{MAP} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \lambda \sum_i w_i^2
\]

This formulation establishes a direct connection between implementation and theory:

- the cross-entropy term corresponds to the negative log-likelihood,
- the L2 penalty corresponds to the negative log-prior,
- and the combined objective corresponds to MAP estimation.

By making this interpretation explicit in both implementation and documentation, the regularization analysis was positioned not only as an empirical comparison, but also as a theoretically grounded investigation.

### 4. Extension of the training and evaluation workflow

The training pipeline was extended to support **flexible yet controlled experimentation** across multiple regularization settings. This included:

- enabling or disabling regularization components through configuration,
- tracking multiple loss components where relevant,
- computing train / validation / test accuracy,
- computing generalization gaps,
- and supporting early stopping with proper checkpoint restoration.

A key goal was to ensure that all experiments produced outputs in a comparable format. This significantly improved the reliability of later aggregation, analysis, and interpretation.

### 5. Logging, persistence, and reproducibility

An additional contribution concerned the **persistence and reproducibility** of the experimental workflow. During development, runtime resets highlighted the need for systematic logging and result storage.

To address this, a persistent logging mechanism was implemented to store:

- full training histories,
- per-epoch metrics,
- final summary metrics,
- and relevant experiment metadata.

This design introduced a clear separation between **training** and **analysis**, making it possible to revisit results, compare experiments, and generate visualizations without retraining from scratch. As a result, the workflow became substantially more robust and reproducible.

### 6. Visualization and comparative analysis

The contribution also included the development of a visualization and comparison layer for the regularization branch. The goal was not simply to run experiments, but to make their behavior analytically interpretable.

This involved the preparation of:

- per-experiment training curves,
- summary tables across methods,
- and comparative plots in which the baseline and regularized models were visualized on the same scale.

These outputs made it possible to examine:

- overfitting behavior,
- training stability,
- generalization improvements,
- and the relative effect of individual versus combined regularization strategies.

In this sense, the contribution extended beyond implementation to the **analytical presentation and interpretation of the experimental results**.

### 7. Repository-level organization and documentation

Beyond the branch-specific experimental work, an important part of the contribution concerned the **overall organization and technical presentation of the repository**. The project was shaped so that it would function as a coherent, technically documented study rather than as a disconnected collection of notebooks.

This included substantial responsibility for the preparation and organization of the main documentation files, particularly:

- **`README.md`**
- **`report.md` / `report.pdf`**

In addition, the repository as a whole was given a clearer structure and presentation style so that the experimental content, folder organization, and written documentation formed a unified project. This aspect was especially important given that evaluation was based not only on implementation, but also on **technical documentation, methodological depth, and clarity of presentation**.

### 8. Overall contribution

Taken together, this contribution established a complete and reproducible framework for the **regularization side of the project**, covering methodology design, implementation, theoretical grounding, experiment management, result persistence, visualization, and documentation.

In concrete terms, the work delivered:

- an intentionally overfitting baseline,
- a controlled comparison framework,
- implementations of multiple regularization methods and combinations,
- a theoretically grounded formulation of L2 regularization as MAP estimation,
- an extended training and evaluation workflow,
- persistent logging and result management,
- comparative visualization and analysis,
- and the preparation of the main project documentation together with the overall repository structure.

Overall, this contribution addressed the following central question:

**How do different regularization strategies affect overfitting and generalization when all other training conditions are kept constant, and how can these findings be integrated into a coherent, technically documented deep learning project?**

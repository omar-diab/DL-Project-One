# Responsibilities and Contributions

My main responsibility in this project was to design and implement the **data augmentation** and **adversarial training** components within the overall regularization pipeline. Both techniques were applied to the same overfitting MLP baseline trained on CIFAR-10, under identical controlled conditions shared across all team experiments.

---

## 1. Data Augmentation (`reg_09_data_augmentation.ipynb`)

I implemented data augmentation as a regularization strategy applied **exclusively to the training set**, preserving the integrity of validation and test evaluation.

### Augmentation techniques applied

- `RandomHorizontalFlip` — introduces horizontal symmetry variations
- `RandomCrop(32, padding=4)` — simulates translation invariance
- `ColorJitter(brightness=0.2, contrast=0.2)` — adds appearance variability

### Design decisions

- Two separate transform pipelines were defined: an augmented one for training and a standard normalization-only pipeline for validation and test. This ensures that augmentation influences only the learning process, not evaluation.
- The CIFAR-10 training set was loaded twice — once with each transform — so that the validation split uses unaugmented data.
- All other hyperparameters (architecture, optimizer, seed, split sizes) remain fixed to the team-wide controlled setup.

### What this experiment measures

The experiment isolates the effect of augmentation on overfitting and generalization by comparing:
- training and validation loss/accuracy curves against the unregularized baseline,
- the generalization gap (train accuracy − validation accuracy) across epochs.

---

## 2. Adversarial Training — FGSM (`reg_10_adversarial_training.ipynb`)

I implemented adversarial training using the **Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM)**, which generates adversarial examples on-the-fly during training and incorporates them into the loss computation.

### Method

At each training step, adversarial examples are generated as:

$$
x_{\text{adv}} = x + \varepsilon \cdot \text{sign}(\nabla_x \mathcal{L}(\theta, x, y))
$$

The combined training loss mixes clean and adversarial contributions equally:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = 0.5 \cdot \mathcal{L}(x) + 0.5 \cdot \mathcal{L}(x_{\text{adv}})
$$

With a perturbation magnitude of $\varepsilon = 0.03$.

### Design decisions

- FGSM attack generation is implemented inside the training loop with `requires_grad` on inputs, ensuring gradients are computed w.r.t. the input rather than the model parameters.
- A dedicated `evaluate_adversarial` function was written to assess robustness at test time, separate from standard clean accuracy evaluation.
- The 3-way split (15 000 train / 5 000 val / 10 000 test) is consistent with all other team experiments.

### What this experiment measures

The experiment evaluates both:
- **generalization** — whether adversarial training affects standard clean accuracy and the train/val gap,
- **robustness** — clean vs. adversarial test accuracy, to quantify resistance to FGSM perturbations.
